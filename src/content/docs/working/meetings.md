Agenda in the invite or decline is a valid response. Before you book time at all, decide whether a meeting is even the right tool — if an email or Slack thread resolves it, send that instead.[^etiquette]

## Before the meeting

- **Meeting or email?** Pick whichever gets to the outcome faster. No agenda you can write down usually means no meeting you should book.
- **Agenda and pre-reads go in the invite.** Concise agenda, any materials attached — people should know why they're there before they accept.
- **Mark attendees required or optional.** Be honest about which is which.
- **Assign roles up front:** facilitator, note taker, and timekeeper.

## During the meeting

- Timebox agenda items where it helps — it's how you confirm you can actually cover everything.
- We are better together: joining on camera is encouraged.
- Mute unless you're speaking; raise the digital hand to get in the queue.
- End on time. Someone else's meeting starts when yours ends.

## After the meeting

The meeting owner sends the notes: key decisions, action items, owners, and due dates. Notes in the doc, decisions in the decision log — a meeting that produced no written outcome may as well not have happened.

## Calendar etiquette

**Organizers:**

- Review your recurring meetings regularly — is the frequency still earning its slot?
- Cap recurring series at **180 days**; re-book deliberately instead of squatting on rooms forever.
- Use the Scheduling Assistant to find a time that works across time zones, and mark required vs. optional.
- Include a Zoom/Teams link for remote folks and a room for on-site attendees when possible.
- Use standard lengths (**25 or 50 minutes**) so people can get to their next meeting. Need more? Schedule a follow-up, don't overrun.

**Participants:**

- Respond to every invite: accept, decline, or tentative. Silence helps nobody plan.
- Declining is fine — tell the owner why when it's useful context.
- Use tentative when you're double-booked or your availability might change.
